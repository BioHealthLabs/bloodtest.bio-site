Blood tests explained — what markers can (and can’t) tell you.

Blood tests are often ordered, then misunderstood. This page explains the most common panels and markers in plain language, and how to think about results safely. This is educational only — not medical advice.

  • Understand common panels like CBC, lipids, HbA1c, thyroid and liver/kidney markers.
  • Learn what “out of range” can mean (and when it can be benign).
  • See “by goal” paths: fatigue, metabolic health, inflammation, hormones and more.
Best practice: interpret results with symptoms + history + repeat tests over time — not one single number.

Common blood test panels (high-level)

Below is a plain-language overview of what these panels typically aim to measure. The same test can be used for different clinical questions — so context matters.

Panel What it’s often used for Typical markers inside Common gotchas
CBC (Complete Blood Count) Red/white blood cell patterns, anemia screening, infection clues RBC, Hgb/Hct, WBC, platelets, MCV/MCH Hydration, recent illness, and inflammation can shift values
Lipid panel Cardiometabolic risk signals Total cholesterol, LDL, HDL, triglycerides Non-fasting vs fasting can change triglycerides
Glucose / HbA1c Diabetes risk and longer-term glucose control Fasting glucose, HbA1c HbA1c can be affected by anemia and red cell turnover
Thyroid basics Thyroid function signals TSH, Free T4 (sometimes Free T3) Timing, illness, and certain meds can move TSH
Liver markers Liver irritation/injury pattern signals ALT, AST, ALP, bilirubin Hard workouts can elevate AST/ALT temporarily
Kidney markers Kidney filtration and hydration context Creatinine, eGFR, BUN Creatinine can be influenced by muscle mass and supplements
CRP Inflammation signal (non-specific) hs-CRP or standard CRP CRP rises with infections, injuries, and many inflammatory states

Choose a goal (how people actually use blood tests)

These are not diagnostic checklists. They are “interpretation maps” to help you understand what a clinician is usually trying to rule in/out and why repeat testing matters.

Goal / symptom Common starting markers Why they’re used What to watch for
Fatigue / low energy CBC, ferritin/iron context, thyroid basics, B12/folate context Anemia patterns, thyroid signals, deficiency signals One abnormal value doesn’t explain fatigue by itself
Metabolic health Fasting glucose, HbA1c, lipid panel Longer-term glucose and cardiometabolic risk signals Focus on trends + lifestyle + meds context
Inflammation / “I feel sick” CBC differential, CRP (context), sometimes liver markers Non-specific inflammation signals + immune cell patterns CRP is non-specific; don’t self-diagnose infections
Hormone questions Thyroid basics; sex hormones only with symptom-specific context Many “hormone panels” are timing-sensitive Time of day, cycle day, and meds can dominate results
Liver / kidney concern ALT/AST/ALP/bilirubin; creatinine/eGFR/BUN Pattern signals, not single-number judgments Exercise, alcohol, dehydration can shift values

How to prepare (simple, practical)

Many “false alarms” come from prep issues: fasting, hydration, supplements, timing, and recent training. Always follow your lab or clinician instructions first.

  • Fasting: often relevant for triglycerides and fasting glucose (your provider will specify).
  • Hydration: dehydration can concentrate values and affect kidney-related markers.
  • Hard training: intense workouts can temporarily raise some markers (especially muscle/liver enzymes).
  • Supplements: disclose common ones (iron, creatine, biotin) because they can affect certain tests.
  • Timing: some hormones and cortisol-related markers vary by time of day.
If you retest, aim to keep conditions similar (time of day, fasting, training, hydration) so trends are meaningful.

Reading results safely

Reference ranges are not universal “healthy vs unhealthy” cutoffs. They are statistical ranges from a population. A result can be “out of range” for benign reasons — or “in range” while a problem exists.

Rule of thumb Why it matters Example
Look for patterns Single markers are noisy; patterns are more informative Low Hgb + low MCV suggests a different story than low Hgb + high MCV
Compare over time Trends are often more meaningful than one-off values HbA1c drifting up slowly is a stronger signal than one slightly high reading
Context beats numbers Symptoms, meds, infections, and training can dominate CRP after a cold means something different than persistent CRP elevation

Limits & common pitfalls

Blood testing is powerful, but not magic. Here’s what this site will always be strict about.

  • Not diagnosis: You cannot diagnose serious disease from a generic panel alone.
  • At-home constraints: finger-prick tests can differ from venous draws for some markers.
  • Over-testing risk: more tests can create more false positives (noise) without clear benefit.
  • Lab variation: different labs and methods can give slightly different results.
If you have concerning symptoms, use a qualified clinician and a properly supervised testing pathway.
